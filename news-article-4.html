2 Teens Busted Trying to Launch Meth into Prison Using a Cannon

By Nemx Cami

July 27,2025,19:45PM

Share :

If the risk don’t scare you,the payoff ain’t real.

“Hustle isn’t pretty.if the fear doesn’t scare you, the payday isn’t worth it.”
Prison contraband isn’t just smuggled :
—it’s engineered. Two teens recently got caught trying to cannon meth into a correctional facility, a move that screams desperation, creativity, and reckless ambition. Meth itself is highly profitable: wholesale prices for pure crystal meth can reach $5,000–$20,000 per pound , depending on location. But smuggling it into a prison? That’s a risk multiplier that borders on suicidal.

Hustle Time

Here’s what this shady operation teaches:
any side hustler willing to stare the abyss: These teens didn’t walk the standard route.They built a cannon—literally engineering a launch system. Side hustles thrive when you improvise outside normal rules, though legality is a boundary most people shouldn’t cross. Smuggling drugs into prison is extremely high-risk, but if it worked, the return could’ve been astronomical. Successful hustlers often balance on the edge between genius and disaster; most people never dare. Prisons are closed systems where demand is sky-high and supply is near-zero. That’s why contraband trades for insane markups. In legal terms, high-demand, low-supply markets are where hustlers make serious cash—think rare sneakers, event tickets, collectibles. Even in shady contexts, the lesson is clear:success often requires calculated risk, ingenuity, and understanding your market—but crossing the line comes with consequences that can destroy your life.

Some Heat

These teens are textbook examples of ambition
untethered from foresight. They’re proof that hustlers will sometimes do whatever it takes, even if it’s insane, illegal, or borderline suicidal. You can admire the audacity while recognizing the stupidity—the difference between a smart hustle and a criminal disaster is knowledge, planning, and moral boundaries.
